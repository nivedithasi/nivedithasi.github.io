# Slum Tourism and The Rhetoric of Dharavi

“We see them daily. We can’t be certain if the sun will shine tomorrow. However, one thing is certain. It’s always certain we will see them.”
<p style="text-align: right;">- Female, 26, housewife from Dharavi [1]</p>

“They” are tourists and their place of visit is one that this woman calls home. Most of the world calls it a slum. This is Dharavi, a locality in Mumbai, reputed to be Asia’s oldest and largest slum. Mumbai is the world’s slum capital and Dharavi is its centerpiece, cramming a million people into a 1.75 sq km triangle. Dharavi has evolved from a blight on an urban metropolis to a socioeconomic accelerator and its transformation has drawn thousands of tourists to its streets in search of this spectacle.

Tourist reviews left on Tripadvisor, an American travel website, suggest that Dharavi slum tours leave an indelible impression on tourists. But how does slum tourism affect Dharavi? I have never been part of a slum tour. So I did the natural thing for our covid era and began visiting the websites of slum tour operators. I was struck by their uncanny resemblance to wildlife tourism websites. How did this likeness come about and where does it end?

Studying wildlife tourism is useful because it is much older than slum tourism, having evolved from hunting expeditions with the camera replacing the gun. Perhaps, examining the impact of wildlife tourism on wildlife habitat will help us predict the future of slums like Dharavi. The ability to make these predictions is critical and timely because slum populations are burgeoning in many parts of Asia, Africa and Latin America. A billion people across the world escape rural poverty by migrating to cities where jobs can be found but not affordable housing and end up living in slums [2]. Many of these slums now play host to tourists,affecting the narrative about their future. This is obviously important for the millions who live in Dharavi and in the other slums of Mumbai. And given Dharavi’s history and international fame, it seems inevitable that its development narrative will affect those of slums in the rest of the world.

## The Changed Perception of Dharavi

Today, Dharavi plays host to a large number of international tourists seeking to see what life is like for slum-dwellers in India. Tourists are taken through narrow alleyways of the sprawling slum to observe the economic and social lives of its residents. In 2019, the last year of pre-Covid global tourism, Dharavi became the favourite tourist experience of India, even besting the Taj Mahal, for the travel site TripAdvisor’s Travellers’ Choice Awards [3].

It’s an ironic twist to a narrative that dominated Asia for most of the twentieth century wherein developing a city required a “slum rehabilitation” program, a euphemism for forced eviction of slum dwellers and their resettlement in shabby, concrete towers located in the city’s outer fringes. Slums, once seen as dirty eyesores that kept away tourists, have now become magnets for foreign tourists curious to know how “the other half” of the world lives [4].

The rhetoric surrounding “the other half” was heavily influenced by the 2008 movie, Slumdog Millionaire. Slum tourism in Dharavi truly took off after the release of the movie, which depicts slums as places that residents long to escape from. However, a different narrative of Dharavi has emerged since from the resulting boom in slum tourism. It is no longer a place of squalid desolation, but a vibrant, industrious community whose members practice artisanal trades such as pottery, climb the value chain of the leather industry and respond in creative ways to the indifference of the state. Pity and revulsion toward slum life have given way to admiration and delight [5].

For a complete analysis of how the rhetoric of slum tourism affects Dharavi and how wildlife tourism can help us predict the future effects of current rhetoric, we need to understand existing research on slum tourism, wildlife tourism and how ‘the tourist gaze’ impacts them.

## The Debate on Slum Tourism

Slum tourism rests on the claim that it benefits the slums much the way tourism benefits a city. It provides employment for tour guides from the slums and an opportunity for the potters and leather workers of Dharavi to sell souvenirs. Many tours are conducted by non-profit organisations that publicise how they contribute a portion of their proceeds towards charity work in Dharavi, although the direct economic impact of all this is insignificant [6]. Scholars such as Fabian Frenzel, defend slum tourism by contending that since slum dwellers lack voice and recognition, tourism provides an audience for stories that need to be told [7].

On the other hand, slum tourism receives criticism for de-contextualising the plight of Dharavi’s poor while empowering privileged travelers [8]. Nisbett finds that tourists overlook the squalor and romanticize Dharavi as “a model of contentment and neighbourliness.”

But even within the conflicting opinions, there is consensus that the relationship of poverty and tourism has changed. In Slum Tourism: State of the Art, Frenzel et al. concede that “tourism is no longer only a means to fight poverty, but poverty is an attraction of tourism” [9]. In doing so, they highlight the dissonance inherent in slum tourism. Its declared objective is to increase awareness about the lives of the poor. But in attempting to show tourists the positive aspects of those lives, it effectively glosses over their adversity and its structural causes.

To demonstrate how this plays out, we can turn to Simon Crerar’s account for London-based The Times. He writes, “The few hours I spend touring Mumbai’s teeming Dharavi slum are uncomfortable and upsetting, teetering on voyeuristic. They are also among the most uplifting of my life” [10]. His motivation for taking the tour goes back to wanting to visit Mumbai after Slumdog Millionaire's Oscar sweep. Reflecting on his assumptions about Dharavi he is surprised that, "Instead of a neighbourhood characterised by misery, [he finds] a bustling and enterprising place, packed with small‐scale industries defying their circumstances to flourish amidst squalor. Rather than pity, [he is] inspired by man’s alchemic ability to thrive when the chips are down" [10]. If this is the effect that a Dharavi slum tour has on a journalist, it is hard to expect the average tourist to come away with a useful understanding of local problems, let alone participate in actions, post-tour, to affect concrete change.

## The Debate on Wildlife Tourism

In a 2015 paper, wildlife conservation researchers at Oxford University draw attention to the trade-offs in wildlife tourism between conservation, animal welfare, visitor satisfaction and profitability [11]. As with slum tourism, the researchers conclude that its adverse effects go unrecognised by the majority of tourists [11]. Wildlife tourism can disturb animals in their natural habitats, especially at sensitive times of their life cycle, and adversely affect their breeding. Feeding by tourists can distort their behaviors and potentially endanger them. Indirect effects include the environmental impact of their lodging and other tourist comforts [12].

A common justification for wildlife tourism is that it packs a powerful combination of economic incentives and emotional appeal, to preserve wildlife. Take the case of Cecil, a large male lion in Zimbabwe, whose fame and following arose from wildlife tourism [13]. His killing during a hunting expedition in 2015 and his widespread fame resulted in international outrage. People around the world would never have known about the killing if not for wildlife tourism and its undertones of preservation. However, extending that preservation logic to slums seems perverse. Slum tourism may not have arisen to preserve slums but once an industry is up and running to meet a perceived demand, it gives rise to incentives for preserving that which sustains it.

Even though they arose under different circumstances and with different goals, slum and wildlife tourism have gradually converged to resemble ecotourism, a concept birthed by conservationists in the 1980s “to channel tourism revenues into support for conservation and local development” [14]. Stronza et al. find that ecotourism has many definitions, but “one clear set of principles [that is] designed to ensure a positive feedback loop between tourism and conservation.” Explicit in all definitions of ecotourism is the hypothesis that tourism, when designed and practiced as ecotourism, can benefit wildlife and biodiversity, create incentives to protect landscapes, and support local communities [14]. Both wildlife tourism and slum tourism are now promoted as low impact, educational tourism. Both emphasize the living, physical and cultural features of the destination and by extension the importance of conserving those features [15].

However, conventional tourism and outdoor recreation often adopt the rhetoric of ecotourism even though these are the very activities for which ecotourism is designed to provide a superior alternative. This unfortunate rebranding is one of the main reasons, Stronza et al. conclude, that despite three decades of research-based evidence on its economic, environmental, and social benefits, ecotourism has been dismissed and critiqued as ineffective.

## The Tourist Gaze

Having considered the conversation on slum and wildlife tourism, it is worth pausing to consider work that analyses how tourists choose to visit particular places and the subsequent impact of their visits. In The Tourist Gaze and the Environment, John Urry chronicles tourism in the ‘West’ [16]. Urry argues that environmental and wildlife tourism results from people seeking “solitude, privacy and a personal, semi-spiritual relationship with their environment.” Slum tourism, however, is driven by a quest for novelty. While it might seem risky to visit an unsanitary, cramped space on holiday, Urry writes that “being a tourist seems to involve some striking changes in what is perceived to be risky…because of the forms of exotic visual consumption that place such activities in a different context from what is everyday.” The ability of a place to deliver something “specific” and “new” has also resulted in tourist agencies “seeking to forge a distinctive image” that will attract capital and tourists [16].

The need to connect with nature and experience novelty have made particular destinations more attractive which has consequently increased “demands to protect or conserve such environments” [16]. If this is the effect of tourism on high-demand destinations, perhaps the presence of tourists in Dharavi could result in the slum’s conservation.

## The Appropriation of Ecology and Nature Writing Tools to Describe Dharavi

In his reflective essay for the New York Times, Jim Yardley describes Dharavi as “layered” and “fragile” [17]. “Every layer of Dharavi,” he argues, “reveals something far more complicated, and organic.” He describes Dharavi as “an informal city as layered as Mr. Mobin’s [a waste recycler] sheared building — and as fragile,” conjuring visions of a tropical rainforest. A rainforest has four layers. There is the forest floor, over it an understory of tree trunks that is covered by a canopy layer, and the final emergent layer on top of the existing canopy. Each layer is a distinct habitat because of the different levels of sunlight and rainfall they receive. Yardley's Dharavi also has four layers. He writes, "One slum. Four layers. Four realities. On the ground floor is misery. One floor up is work. Another floor up is politics. And at the top is hope."

For him, “a walk through Dharavi is a journey through a dank maze of ever-narrowing passages until the shanties press together so tightly that daylight barely reaches the footpaths below, as if the slum were a great urban rain forest, covered by a canopy of smoke and sheet metal” [17]. This is the new narrative of Dharavi, that incorporates language traditionally associated with wildlife habitats.

The website of Reality Tours, a major operator of slum tours in Dharavi, quotes Suraj, one of its tour guides: "Dharavi recycles about 80% of Mumbai’s plastic waste. The commercial side of Dharavi may look dirty but that’s because they clean the dirt of the city” [18]. In her 2014 book, The Durable Slum, Liza Weinstein warns that if Dharavi “was to disappear,” Mumbai would “cease to function”. Without Dharavi’s recycling industry, Mumbai would “choke,” declares Green is the new Black, a website promoting sustainability [19]. It’s as though Dharavi were a keystone species propping up the Mumbai ecosystem. But do these rhetorical similarities extend beyond press reportage and general discussions?

## Rhetoric of Tour Operators and Tourists in the Context of Slum and Wildlife Tourism

To further explore these parallels, we can turn to websites of Dharavi slum tour operators and wildlife tour operators. Both offer “authentic” sights of something that has naturally evolved. Both target the same type of tourist - adventure-seekers who consider themselves informed and ethical. Therefore, their websites use similar methods of persuasion.

In Mirissa, a popular Sri Lankan spot for whale watching, websites such as Raja & the Whales and Whale Watching Club promote themselves as “responsible” tour operators. Raja & the Whales devotes several webpages to “Research & Conservation” [20]. Reality Tours, the largest slum tour operator in Dharavi, describes its tours as “ethical and educational” [21]. It portrays Dharavi as a pinnacle of street fashion, references how tourists have been “inspired” by their visit and declares that eighty percent of the proceeds go to charities operating in Dharavi [18, 22].

The effectiveness of this commercial messaging is borne out by the descriptions of tourists themselves and demonstrates the shift in attitudes towards slum tourism. On The Wandering Quinn, a travel blog about a “travel addict’s” experience, the author comments “as much as I wanted to do a slum tour, I had a feeling participating in slum tourism in India was the wrong thing to do” [23]. She adds, “After all, I didn't want to go and stare at thousands of people’s poverty, feel helpless and then leave back to my privileged life.” Nevertheless, she did travel to Mumbai because, “after looking into Reality Tours who put 80% of their profit back into the slum, [she] knew going with them would be a good decision and [she] would be contributing to slum tourism in a positive way” [23].

A similar change of mind unfolds in Something Of Freedom, another travel blog, that recognizes but then qualifies ethical concerns with whale watching in Mirissa. The authors write, “Mirissa has started to get a bit of a bad reputation for offering unethical whale watching tours, but this doesn’t mean all the tours are as bad as each other [24]. They express their ethical concerns with the tour by stating that “[since] the tour is based around wanting to see these amazing animals in the wild, it’s important to put their welfare first.” Because Whale Watching Club supports and practices “ethical whale watching”, the blog goes on to strongly recommend Whale Watching Club tours [24].

## Satire on Wildlife and Slum Tourism

Another common tour commercial messaging tactic is the portrayal of tour experiences as a slice of “real” life. As with Reality Tours, sometimes we need to look no further than the name of a tour agency to be reminded of their mission to deliver “authentic” experiences. But we already know that slum tourism presents a skewed, selective reality. At these crossroads, I am reminded of Barbara W. Tuchman’s quote, “Satire is a wrapping of exaggeration around a core of reality” [25].

Gully Boy, a 2019 Hindi film, is a contemporary take on life in Dharavi and reflects the new narrative. The movie’s protagonist is Murad, an aspiring rapper and long-time Dharavi resident [26]. Early in the film, we watch a group of tourists being directed by their tour guide into Murad’s home. The foreigners marvel at the cramped living spaces and comment on the efficient use of space in wide-eyed amazement. While one woman clicks photos of Murad’s home, another tourist approaches Murad who is watching all this while leaning on his narrow bed. Murad compliments the tourist on his shirt and the tourist explains that the portrait on his shirt is of the famous American rapper, Nas. On cue, Murad begins to recite the lyrics of Nas’s NY State of Mind. Amazed by Murad’s knowledge of an American rap artist, the man asks Murad if he can photograph him. Murad doesn't reply but he covers his face with his hand. The man clicks a photo anyway [26].

Creature Comforts is an animated series by Aardman Animations that uses human voice overs for clay-model animals in stop motion animation videos [27]. Their speciality is using audio from mundane conversations recorded in and around London and deploying those dialogues with comic timing to animal life settings. In one clip, the animators use a recording of a traffic policeman moving cars away from a space as the backing track for a gorilla at a natural park shooing tourists away [27]. Both these scenes, from Gully Boy and Creature Comforts, portray tourists as insensitive and intrusive. Both depict residents’ displeasure at being photographed and their subsequent attempts to evade ‘tourist gaze’. Both are fictional works, and despite the obvious difference that one is an explicit caricature, both resonate with the viewer. Importantly, both films are widely available on streaming platforms, enabling them to shape public discourse.

## Conclusion

From the advertising style of slum and wildlife tour operators to tourist reviews that reflect on “life changing” experiences, the rhetoric of slum tourism is growing ever closer to that of wildlife tourism. Recent accounts of Dharavi describe the slum as a fragile habitat that has grown organically from economic and community needs, layer upon layer, like a tropical rainforest. Research papers on slum tourism echo scholarly critiques of wildlife tourism. Even the movie parodies of these two forms of tourism are strikingly similar.

We can already see the new rhetoric of Dharavi being employed by those seeking to influence the future of Dharavi. In a 2018 TEDx video arguing against Dharavi’s demolition, Matias Echanove and Rahul Srivastav of the research collective Partners for Urban Knowledge Action and Research say that “Dharavi has grown through very special relationships between economic activity, family needs, community needs.” [28]. Dharavi is portrayed as an organic, growing and evolving body that deserves to persist.

Few will question the value of preserving wildlife habitats and perhaps even expanding them. What about Dharavi and other slums around the world? While nobody is calling for the expansion of slums, are they best left untouched so that they may continue to evolve? Or should they be demolished in the name of improved living standards for all? If indeed Dharavi is like a wildlife habitat whose structural features, constituents and their activities are all tightly interlinked, then that dims the prospect of a middle way; a transformation of Dharavi that somehow rids it of all its squalor while preserving all the goodness of its socioeconomic fabric.

The rhetoric that slum tourism has helped create will affect the well-being of millions of people of Dharavi and Mumbai at large. And it will have implications for millions more in slums across the world. In the midst of the trepidation caused by the potential redevelopment of Dharavi, acknowledging and analysing the parallels of rhetoric between slum tourism and wildlife tourism could be the first step towards making predictions and framing discussions about Dharavi’s future.
