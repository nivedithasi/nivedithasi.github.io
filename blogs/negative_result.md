# Learning Well from Being Wrong

## The unavailability of negative and null results in science leads to wasted time and resources in experimentation. It also skews scientific theory to fitting positive results and ignoring what didn't work. Scientific progress will accelerate if we incentivise sharing negative results and the precise experiment configurations that generated them. While this idea is not new, previous attempts to address this with dedicated journals have struggled due to structural barriers and disincentives. This essay argues that in our new world of agent-backed science, releasing negative results is achievable and necessary and proposes an alternative approach to collecting this data.

{dc}Four years ago, as a summer research intern at a drug discovery lab, I had three months to build a pharmacokinetic prediction pipeline for small molecules. My task was to model the absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion and toxicity (collectively "ADMET") of candidate drugs. ADMET is an important screening criterion for candidate compounds towards the long and expensive process of drug development.

As a beginner scientist, I had many ideas and an acute shortage of time and compute to test them. If I had a magic wand that summer, I'd seek to quickly shrink my experiment list by scouring research archives for all ideas that had been tested and their results, not just the relatively few true positives.

The reasons for sharing research failures are well understood. There is value in reporting errors that turn out to be about a system or tool ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/nature-index/news/three-reasons-to-share-your-research-science-failures)). Sharing unsuccessful work at scale saves resources by eliminating unproductive approaches for the community ([Reddit thread](https://www.reddit.com/r/research/comments/1hty986/hot_take_journals_should_publish_failed/)). And failures improve overall understanding. After all, a theory that is built mostly on positive results is likely to be less complete than one that can also properly account for the failures.

Then why do negative results rarely find their way into publications? For starters, there is neither new funding nor fame in publishing them and a perceived reputational risk in making failures public. Writing clean reports that neatly explain failures takes time. And existing research journals are not positioned to handle the large volume of reported failures. Attempts through specialized journals, such as the [Journal of Negative Results](https://www.jnr-eeb.org/index.php/jnr) among others, have often failed due to difficulties in attracting submissions. While [PLOS ONE](https://collections.plos.org/collection/missing-pieces/) and [F1000Research](https://f1000research.com/) do maintain active collections for such findings, it can be challenging for researchers who must stay abreast of all the successful results in their field, to also cope with the much larger domain of failed results.


{sep}

Advances in cloud computing and agentic AI now allow the storage, retrieval and ingestion of datasets that far exceed human capacity. As an AI researcher, I can now use tools like [Weights & Biases](https://wandb.ai/) to track every experiment I ever launch and [GitHub](https://github.com/) to trace every model and code change made. Not only are the negative results available, the precise experiment configuration and execution that led to the result is also easily documented. The prevailing positive-result mindset has us release a snapshot of a clean codebase devoid of all its incremental tweaks, a select set of positive experiment plots and a compelling research story. Instead, we could release it all.

I propose the construction of result datasets where each record is a single experiment-result pair, optionally linked to an associated paper (there may be no such paper). Many of these pairs of which will be negative or null results. Instead of a journal-style curation of negative result papers, we will mantain a simple collection of experiment-result pairs. Researchers can always choose to provide written analyses of their negative results in their publications.

Today, human researchers can outsource configuring research record tools and consistently tracking code changes to tireless agents. On the consumption front, autonomous systems can also parse massive experiment records to identify, cluster and analyze prior experiment attempts. For the first time, it is easy to record and usefully consume negative results! Beyond computing and computational research, my conversations with peers in the physical and life sciences have convinced me that in due time this can extend to science as a whole.

Agentic research amplifies the necessity of negative results. Even when agents save us time today, we still confront resource constraints on data, money, approvals etc. Without negative results to direct action-biased agents, we will observe severe inefficiencies with repeated work. I also expect that the number of new observations and discoveries will increase, leading to ever more theories that explain them. However, many of these theories will fit the positive samples (à la [Law of Truly Large Numbers](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/LawofTrulyLargeNumbers.html)). Requiring theories to explain negative results will enforce stronger guardrails on scientific slop.

{sep}

To test the utlity of our null result collection, we will select a representative sample of computational researchers in a specific domain (e.g. drug design for a particular pathway or sparse LLM architecture design), so that researchers benefit from peer results. Over a span of three years (assuming paper life cycles span 6-18 months), the researchers will agree to use recording tools that ambiently record every experiment-result pair. We will launch and mantain the service that houses these records. Next, we will continually log instances where researchers chose not to run an experiment due to evidence of prior failure and estimate the resource savings (this can be self-reported or estimated by an agent monitor).

At fixed intervals, we will have this experiment group and a control group self-evaluate productivity and research novelty levels. For a "double-blind", a separate set of independent researchers will evaluate the groups' producitivity and idea generation. Care will be taken to anonymize records to minimize bias and externalities to all participants. Material savings and statistically significant improvmements in productivity and novelty against the control group (this is especially useful when agents keep improving) should provide evidence in favor recording negative results.

Retrying "failed" science is helpful because minor method adjustments can materially change outcomes. Existing negative results might dissuade scientists from revisiting hypotheses. Luckily, since we can record the precise experiment configurations and decisions that led to the negative results, humans and agents can exercise judgement to test variations. Bad actors could fake negative results to dissuade peers from pursuing promising ideas. Here too, the exact trace of a negative result helps. Every negative result collection will support reproducibility engines that randomly re-run submissions to surface wrongdoing and lower public credibility scores.

This new world presents new incentives. We can make recording null results painless. Then, novel discoveries will increasingly rest on prior successes and failures (either in agent training data or what data was used for discovery). Since negative result collections save resources and seed new ideas and can easliy be cited, new works would reference both the positive and negative experiment-result pairs that influenced experiment directions. Thus cementing the incentives for researchers to publish their null results and experimental failures.
